## Abstract (from the initial description for this topic).

The Manhattan Project was a top-secret U.S.-led research initiative during World War II that developed the world’s first nuclear weapons. It began in 1939 in response to fears that Nazi Germany was working on an atomic bomb. The project brought together some of the greatest scientific minds of the era, including Enrico Fermi, Niels Bohr, and Richard Feynman.

The multiple sites of the project included Los Alamos (New Mexico), Oak Ridge (Tennessee), and Hanford (Washington). Each site focused on different aspects of nuclear research—from uranium enrichment to plutonium production to weapon design. The scientists faced immense technical and theoretical challenges, but by July 1945 they had succeeded. The first nuclear explosion, known as the Trinity test, was detonated in the New Mexico desert, proving that an atomic bomb was feasible.

Less than a month later, two atomic bombs were dropped on the Japanese cities of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945, leading to Japan's surrender and the end of World War II. The Manhattan Project not only ushered in the nuclear age but also marked a turning point in science, warfare, and geopolitics. It raised profound ethical questions about the use of such weapons and established the foundation for postwar nuclear arms races and the Cold War. The project's legacy continues to shape global security and energy policy today.




# New focus:<br/>Science in Time of War<br/>Discovery, Power, and Moral Responsibility



How should we judge scientific work when the same discovery can feed the hungry, end a war, deter aggression, or open the door to mass destruction and political abuse?

War does not simply “use” science; it reshapes the priorities, speed, secrecy, and moral vocabulary of science itself. In each case—Haber, Fermi, and Amodei—we see a scientist or scientific leader confronting the same dilemma in different forms: **when does serving national necessity become helping build tools that escape moral control?**



## Why science and war are natural partners — and dangerous ones


War accelerates science because states reward research that promises advantage: more food, more energy, better communications, stronger materials, faster computation, deadlier weapons.

### The paradox

The same scientific breakthrough can appear:

* humane in one context,
* patriotic in another,
* catastrophic in a third.

### Key themes for the whole talk

1. **Dual use** — discoveries that help civilization and warfare at once.
2. **Responsibility** — what scientists owe society once uses become destructive.
3. **Control problem** — political institutions often cannot fully control what science unleashes.
4. **Legacy problem** — the moral meaning of a discovery changes over time.


The three paradigms span a century:

* chemistry in industrial war,
* physics in total war,
* artificial intelligence in an era of autonomous systems.



## Case Study 1: Fritz Haber and World War I

### Summary of Haber's 1920 Nobel Prize Lecture

In his Nobel lecture, Haber presents ammonia synthesis as both a scientific and civilizational achievement. He begins with a deceptively simple reaction—nitrogen plus hydrogen yields ammonia—but argues that chemists long failed to make it work at useful scale because the reaction required unusually strict conditions, specialized apparatus, and careful thermodynamic analysis. He frames his work not as a lucky discovery but as the outcome of combining theory with experimental persistence.

A major part of the lecture explains why ammonia mattered so much. Haber stresses that modern agriculture depends on “fixed” nitrogen, yet plants cannot use atmospheric nitrogen directly. Traditional sources such as Chilean saltpeter were limited, and industrial society was removing nitrogen from soils faster than natural cycles could replenish it. For Haber, this made synthetic nitrogen a pressing economic and agricultural necessity.

He then surveys earlier failed or insufficient approaches, including nitrogen fixation through electrical discharge and indirect chemical routes such as cyanamide. These methods either consumed too much energy or were not scalable enough. That pushed him back toward the direct synthesis of ammonia from nitrogen and hydrogen.

The heart of the lecture is technical but closes by emphasizing the larger payoff: synthetic ammonia could relieve fears of nitrogen scarcity and bring “new nutritive riches to mankind.”

**Source:** [Haber's Nobel Lecture](https://www.nobelprize.org/uploads/2018/06/haber-lecture.pdf) 1920.

### Why Haber matters

Haber is perhaps the clearest example of the dual-use scientist. 

In this book *The Alchemy of Air,* Thomas Hager writes that Haber and his partner Carl Bosch “made bread from air,” but the same chemistry fed the machinery of modern war.

### The constructive side

* The Haber-Bosch process made synthetic ammonia possible.
* That transformed fertilizer production and helped sustain modern agriculture.
* Historians and scientists widely credit synthetic nitrogen with supporting global population growth by dramatically increasing crop yields. 

### The destructive side

* The same chemistry also fed the explosives economy of industrial war.
* During World War I, Haber became closely associated with Germany’s chemical weapons program and supported the first large-scale chlorine gas attack at Ypres in 1915. 

### Moral tension

* Does feeding millions outweigh helping gas thousands?
* Can we separate discovery from the scientist’s later choices?
* Is patriotism a moral excuse in wartime science?

### The Zyklon shadow

* Haber was not directly responsible for the later Nazi use of Zyklon B in genocide.
* But his legacy became morally entangled with later chemical killing technologies.
* That makes him a powerful example of how a scientific legacy can be reinterpreted by later political horrors.



Haber shows that **scientific genius does not protect against moral blindness**.
He did not merely discover chemistry that others misused; he actively embraced chemical warfare as service to the state.


If Haber shows chemistry being folded into industrial war, Fermi shows physics being folded into total war—and into a permanent condition of nuclear anxiety.



## Case Study 2: Enrico Fermi, the atomic bomb, and the nuclear age

### Why Fermi matters

Fermi stands at the intersection of pure science and state power:

* neutron physics,
* the first controlled nuclear chain reaction,
* the Manhattan Project,
* the birth of the nuclear age.

### Wartime justification

* The standard argument: Nazi Germany might build the bomb first.
* In that frame, atomic research became a race the Allies believed they could not afford to lose.
* The Manhattan Project therefore appeared as a grim necessity of total war.

### The moral argument in favor

* The bomb is often defended as a means of ending World War II faster.
* One can argue that scientific participation was justified by the goal of defeating fascism and preventing an even more devastating prolonged war.

### The moral argument against

* Hiroshima and Nagasaki made civilian annihilation technologically scalable.
* Nuclear weapons did not disappear after victory; they created a new geopolitical order built on deterrence, brinkmanship, and possible extinction.
* The Federation of American Scientists notes that today nine states possess nuclear weapons, with the United States and Russia still holding the overwhelming majority of warheads.

### Framing point

**Yes, the bomb may have helped win WWII.
But “winning” also produced a world in which humanity permanently lives under nuclear threat.**

### Discussion questions

* Is success in war enough to morally justify creating a civilization-ending technology?
* Are scientists responsible only for wartime necessity, or also for postwar consequences?
* Did Fermi and his colleagues end one emergency by creating a permanent one?


Fermi is less a villain figure than Haber; that is what makes him useful.
He represents the scientist who can plausibly say:

* the enemy was monstrous,
* the stakes were existential,
* the decision was tragic but necessary.


With Haber and Fermi, the problem was not merely invention but scale: science gave states new powers of death. With AI, the problem shifts again: not just bigger weapons, but systems that may act at machine speed with weak human oversight.



## Case Study 3: Dario Amodei, AI, and the question of autonomous warfare

A live dispute over whether frontier AI systems should be available for autonomous weapons or mass surveillance.


## Machines of Loving Grace 

In “Machines of Loving Grace,” Dario Amodei argues that people underestimate not only AI’s risks but also its potential upside. He says his focus on safety is not because he is pessimistic about AI, but because reducing catastrophic risk is, in his view, what stands between society and a profoundly positive future.

The essay asks readers to imagine the world 5–10 years after the arrival of “powerful AI”: systems smarter than top human experts across many fields, able to use digital tools, work autonomously for long stretches, run in millions of copies, and operate much faster than humans. Amodei calls this a “country of geniuses in a datacenter.”

He rejects both extremes: neither an instant sci-fi “singularity” nor a future where superhuman intelligence changes very little. Instead, he argues that AI progress will be shaped by bottlenecks such as the speed of experiments, missing data, physical limits, legal and social constraints, and the sheer complexity of the world. His key conceptual move is to treat intelligence as a factor of production whose impact depends on what other constraints remain.

Amodei then sketches five domains where AI could dramatically improve human life: biology and physical health, neuroscience and mental health, economic development and poverty, peace and governance, and work and meaning. He is especially optimistic about biology, arguing that AI could accelerate research on disease, lifespan, and everyday health problems, even if progress remains constrained by the pace of real-world experiments and clinical systems.

Overall, the essay is a deliberately hopeful counterpart to AI doom narratives: Amodei’s claim is that the point of taking AI risk seriously is not fear for its own sake, but preserving the possibility of a genuinely better future.

**Source:** [Dario Amodei, “Machines of Loving Grace,” 2024](https://www.darioamodei.com/essay/machines-of-loving-gracedei)


### The basic setup

Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic, has publicly argued for stronger AI safeguards and has resisted certain military uses of advanced AI. In March 2026 that Amodei resisted allowing Claude to be used for domestic mass surveillance or autonomous weapons, and that this became part of a major dispute with the Trump administration and the Pentagon.

This is the contemporary version of the same question:
What happens when the relevant scientific frontier is not chemistry or nuclear physics, but machine intelligence?


### The important nuance

This is **not** a simple pacifist case.
Amodei is not opposed to all AI-enabled military use in principle; rather, he has argued that today’s systems are not reliable enough for certain high-stakes autonomous weapon functions. 

That nuance actually strengthens your outline:

* he is not rejecting national defense,
* he is drawing a line at poorly supervised or insufficiently controllable uses.

### Why this matters politically

Anthropic’s refusal to relax safeguards contributed to a confrontation with the Pentagon, which designated the firm a “supply-chain risk” for DoD contracting. Anthropic is challenging the move in court. 

### The larger philosophical issue

AI changes the war-science relationship in three ways:

1. **Speed**
   Decisions may be made or operationalized too fast for meaningful human review.

2. **Opacity**
   Even developers may not fully understand how a model reaches a particular output.

3. **Diffusion**
   Unlike nuclear weapons, AI capabilities can spread through software, infrastructure, and adaptation rather than rare fissile material alone.

 
* Is building AI for war fundamentally different from building earlier weapons?
* Is “human in the loop” a real safeguard or a comforting slogan?
* Do AI developers have a duty to refuse some state demands?
* When private companies build frontier technologies, who decides the moral limits: engineers, executives, voters, or the national security state?

 
Amodei’s case lets you ask whether we are witnessing a new type of scientist:
not the lone chemist or physicist in a state lab, but the **private-sector scientific entrepreneur** who must decide how much power to hand over to governments.

## Recap so far

Haber embraced weaponization.
Fermi accepted it under extreme wartime necessity.
Amodei, at least in this reported dispute, appears to be trying to draw boundaries before the technology becomes normal military infrastructure.



## Comparative section: what changes across the three paradigms?

### What stays the same

In all three cases:

* science promises national advantage,
* war compresses moral deliberation,
* scientists are told the emergency justifies exceptional action,
* later generations inherit the consequences.

### What changes

#### Haber: chemistry

* tangible substances,
* industrial production,
* direct bodily suffering,
* clear state control.

#### Fermi: nuclear physics

* immense destructive concentration,
* strategic deterrence,
* long-term global instability,
* elite state monopoly, at least initially.

#### Amodei: AI

* software-based capability,
* rapid scaling,
* uncertain boundaries between civilian and military use,
* mixed governance between corporations and states.

### Useful comparison question

Is the moral problem getting worse because the technologies are getting more powerful,
or because human institutions are getting worse at controlling them?


## Big themes

### Dual use is not an exception; it is the rule

Most major scientific advances can be used for both flourishing and destruction.

### Intentions are not enough

Scientists often defend themselves by citing good intentions, but outcomes matter too.

### Patriotism can clarify duty — or excuse complicity

War makes scientists feel that refusal is betrayal. That pressure is central to all three cases.

### The scientist is never “just the inventor”

Once the uses are foreseeable, moral innocence becomes harder to claim.

### The modern problem may be governance, not discovery

The central question may no longer be “should this be invented?” but “who sets the limits, and can those limits hold under pressure?”


## Conclusion



The history of science in wartime is not a story of evil science corrupting pure knowledge. It is a story of human beings using knowledge under conditions of fear, ambition, patriotism, and survival.


Haber, Fermi, and Amodei illuminate three different responses to the militarization of knowledge:

* **embrace,**
* **justify,**
* **resist or constrain.**


When science gives us the power to act at unprecedented scale, is the real problem the technology itself—or our recurring willingness to call almost anything necessary in wartime?
